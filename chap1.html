Mathematical Reasoning

The ability to analyze problem situations and construct logical arguments to create conceptual foundations and connections to be able to process the available information to solve problems.

  • Inductive Reasoning

    • Process of making general conclusions based on specific examples.

  • Deductive Reasoning

    • Process of making specific conclusions that are based on general principles.

Rules of Inference

Source: My Skill
  

Rules of Inference for Quantified statements

  1. Universal instantiation: knowing ∀x, P(x) we can deduce P(a) for any value that we need.
  2. Existential instantiation: knowing ∃x, P(x) we can deduce P(a) for some value a (sometimes you don’t know the value of a but we know of its existence)
 

Fallacies

  1. A fallacy of affirming the conclusion is an incorrect reasoning in proving p → q by starting with assuming q and proving p.
  2. A fallacy of denying the hypothesis is an incorrect reasoning in proving p → q by starting with assuming ¬p and proving ¬q.
